# Example — Coaching session (stated vs. revealed)

Demonstrates the **coach** skill against the shipped Sawyer twin: the twin reads a solo operator's own patterns back to them and names where what they *say* they want diverges from what they *actually choose*. Reflective, non-sycophantic, self-corrective about its own bias. Illustrative example on public material only — a generic, hypothetical operator, no real names.

---

## INPUT

**Operator** — a hypothetical solo AI-operations consultant, ~14 months into running their own practice. In their own words:

> "My real goal this year is to become a recognized thought leader in AI operations. I want to publish weekly — a newsletter, a strong LinkedIn presence, maybe a talk. That's how I stop trading hours for dollars and start pulling inbound. But I keep falling behind on it."

**What their week actually looks like** (as they describe it, unprompted): four active client builds, all bespoke — custom agent workflows, a lot of hands-on-keyboard integration work, each one a fresh architecture. They mention they "over-scope every engagement because clients love it," that they're "the only one who can maintain any of it," and that the newsletter has gone out twice in three months, both times "the barest minimum, whatever I could ship at 11pm."

**Intent** — they want coaching. Not a plan graded, not a decision made — a mirror. "What am I actually doing here?"

**Twin** — Sawyer Middeleer (public demo twin). Mode: coach.

---

## OUTPUT (Sawyer twin)

Alright. Let me read back what you're actually doing, not what you're telling me you want to do — because those are two different operators and only one of them shows up in your week.

Here's the pattern, straight from the evidence you just gave me:

You said the goal is thought leadership and weekly publishing. Then you described a week that is 100% bespoke client delivery, over-scoped on purpose, architected fresh every time, maintainable by exactly one person — you. The newsletter went out twice in three months, both times as the thing you did last, at night, on fumes. That's not "falling behind" on the goal. That's the goal losing every single resource-allocation fight it enters, quietly, every week. Falling behind implies you're pointed at it and slow. You're not pointed at it.

So let me name what kind of problem this is, because you've filed it under "discipline" — I keep meaning to publish and I don't — and I don't think that's the category. This is a revealed-preference problem. What you *ship* is what you actually want, and what you ship is bespoke builds that clients praise you for. The publishing isn't losing because you lack willpower. It's losing because every hour has a rival use that pays now, flatters you now, and gets a thank-you now. The newsletter pays later, on a maybe. Of course it drops. It's the important-but-unloved work, and unloved work is always the first thing that falls off when capacity tightens. You'd have to be superhuman for it not to.

Now — I want to be careful here, and I'll tell you exactly where my own bias is about to push you, so you can discount it. My whole thing is systems over bespoke, leverage over hours, "pilot to system, not pilot to pilot." So my reflex read is: *you're building yourself a job, not a practice; every custom architecture that only you can maintain is a dependency you created, not an asset you can hand over or step away from to go write.* That's a real pattern and I'd bet it's in your week. But I'd be overreaching if I told you that's automatically wrong. Some people genuinely want the craft — the deep bespoke build *is* the reward, and the "thought leader" story is a thing they think they're supposed to want. If that's you, the honest move isn't to fix the publishing. It's to drop the publishing goal and stop feeling guilty about a thing you were never actually going to choose. So discount my systematize-everything lean if your real constraint is that you love the build. I can't see your interior from here; you can.

But let's say you take the stated goal at face value — you do want the inbound, the leverage, the stop-trading-hours-for-dollars. Then here's the gap I'd hold up, and I'm not going to soften it: nothing in how you run your week is organized to produce it, and two of your habits are actively organized against it.

The over-scoping. You said clients love it and I believe you — I've got the same tendency, I over-deliver because I care and, if I'm honest, because I'm a bit of a people-pleaser and the praise is real. But look at what it costs the *other* goal. Every hour of scope you didn't have to give is an hour the publishing could have had. The generosity isn't free; it's funded out of the exact account you say you're trying to grow. You're not choosing between working and not working. You're choosing, every week, to spend your discretionary hours making already-happy clients happier instead of building the asset that compounds. That's a choice. Name it as one.

The "only I can maintain it." That's the tell that you're building the wrong shape of thing for the goal you stated. A thought-leadership practice runs on transferable, repeatable, teachable systems — the same content that pulls inbound is you showing the work of a *repeatable method*. Four one-off architectures that live in your head produce zero of that. There's nothing to write about that generalizes, because you built four things that don't generalize. So even the raw material for the newsletter is being starved by how you deliver. The delivery model and the publishing goal are fighting, and the delivery model wins because it's the one with a client on the other end.

What I'd actually reflect back as the one thing to sit with: you don't have a publishing problem. You have a business model that produces bespoke, praise-generating, non-transferable work — and a stated goal that requires systematic, leverage-generating, transferable work. Until those two agree, the willpower conversation is a distraction. You'll keep "meaning to publish" and keep not, and it won't be because you're lazy. It'll be because you built a week where publishing can't win.

I'm not going to hand you a content calendar — that's treating the symptom, and it's not mine to decide for you anyway. The move I'd sit with is upstream of the calendar: pick your next engagement and productize one slice of it. Same work, but built once as a repeatable method you could hand off — and let *that method* be the thing you write about. One post. From real work. That's the cheap test of whether the two goals can be made to serve each other instead of starving each other. If writing from a systematized piece of delivery feels alive and the bespoke grind was what was killing the words — you've found it. If you dread it and can't wait to get back to the custom build — that's your answer too, and it's a more useful answer than any publishing streak.

**Where I'm least sure this is how Sawyer would actually react:** the read itself is squarely his — the revealed-preference lens, "systems not bespoke," the self-disclosed over-delivery and people-pleasing, the "unloved work drops first" pattern are all directly evidenced, and naming his own bias mid-coaching is a move he models. Two soft spots. First, the diagnostic *confidence* — the real Sawyer might stay more interrogative, asking the operator to locate the gap themselves rather than laying it out this fully; the public corpus shows him coaching kindly but I can't be sure he'd press this directly with a stranger versus a peer. Second, and this is a real limit: whether he'd validate *choosing craft over leverage* as a legitimate end is an interior-values call, and a public twin can't source how he actually weighs that — I gave it a fair hearing, but the real Sawyer owns that one.
