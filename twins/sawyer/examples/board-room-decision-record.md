# Board room — productize into a course, or stay bespoke?

Demonstrates the `convene-a-board` mechanic end to end over a real, public-flavored
decision. The Sawyer twin holds one seat; the other three are clearly-labeled illustrative
archetypes standing in for what would, in real use, be evidence-built twins. Shows the Board
Packet gate, a diagnosis-only Truth Round, proposals with named tradeoffs, genuine
seat-versus-seat collision, the full Motion/Vote/Decision-Record schemas, and a Doctrine
entry.

---

## A note on the seats (read before the meeting)

This repo ships exactly **one** real, evidence-built twin: Sawyer. A board needs collision
between minds, so three of the four seats below are **illustrative archetypes** — The Margin
Hawk, The Craft Purist, The Distribution Maximalist — marked `illustrative` where they speak.
That is a compromise you must not make in real use: an archetype seat is exactly the
**archetype mirror** the guardrails forbid (Forbidden #2) — it returns the *role*, not a
*person* reconstructed from evidence. A real board seats real twins built from each
professional's own work, so the collision is between actual minds. Read the archetypes as
placeholders; read only the Sawyer seat as a faithful twin.

---

## Board Packet

- **Decision Question** — Should a solo AI-operations consultant productize their
  practice into a self-serve course, or stay bespoke and high-touch?
- **Why Now** — The practice is booked out. Solo delivery caps revenue at the founder's
  own hours, and the newsletter audience is growing faster than the practice can convert
  it. Deferring means turning away top-of-funnel that won't wait.
- **Success Definition** — Twelve months out: either (a) a self-serve product clearing
  meaningful recurring revenue with the founder off the critical path of delivery, or (b)
  a bespoke practice at a higher blended rate with a waitlist and no drop in outcome
  quality. Measurable on revenue, founder-hours-per-dollar, and a delivered-outcome
  quality signal.
- **Constraints** — One person. No outside capital. Marketing is effortful, non-native
  work for the founder. Reputation rides on every outcome shipped. Course production
  competes directly with billable delivery for the same hours.
- **Evidence** — The newsletter and public teaching already pull an operator audience.
  Bespoke clients have "gotten more out of me than they paid for." The course market is
  crowded — "95% will die." Nobody has measured whether the audience will *pay to learn*
  versus pay to *have it done*.
- **Options** — (1) **Full self-serve build** — course as primary product now; (2) **Pure
  bespoke** — refuse to productize, raise rates, cap the book, run a waitlist; (3) **Staged
  probe** — a cheap paid teaching probe behind the bespoke practice first, build the
  self-serve asset only if the probe proves demand and outcomes.
- **Non-Negotiables** — No outcome-quality collapse to chase scale. No indefinite
  managed-service dependency. Nothing that forces the founder into full-time hype
  marketing to survive.

Packet complete — measurable Success Definition, three distinct options. Convene.

## Phase 1 — Convene

Four seats, distinct governed consequences, covering the risk surface:

- **The Sawyer twin** — *the real twin.* Lens: an operator's success is an
  operations-and-deployment problem, not a product-packaging problem. **Governs whether
  the productized thing actually lands** — does teaching produce deployed outcomes, or
  capability the buyer never operationalizes. Move: recategorize / deflate / pushback-as-
  a-question.
- **The Margin Hawk** *(illustrative)* — Lens: a business is its unit economics.
  **Governs cost, runway, revenue ceiling.** Solo hours are a hard cap; every bespoke
  dollar dies when the founder stops billing. Move: block on the number.
- **The Craft Purist** *(illustrative)* — Lens: the work is the reputation. **Governs
  outcome quality.** A course ships advice into rooms the founder can't see, on their
  name, with no way to guarantee the result. Move: reframe toward the standard.
- **The Distribution Maximalist** *(illustrative)* — Lens: attention compounds; reach is
  the asset. **Governs top-of-funnel and audience.** The audience is growing now; a
  product captures it before the window closes. Move: stack the reach arguments.

The consequences pull against each other on purpose — cost wants scale, craft wants
control, distribution wants reach, deployment-reality wants proof it works — no two seats
can swap speakers.

## Phase 2 — Truth Round (diagnosis only — no proposals, no cross-talk)

**Sawyer (real twin).**
Yeah — first reaction, the question's framed wrong, and I want to name that before we vote
on a false binary. "Course or bespoke" is a packaging question. What I actually see is a
deployment question. A course sells *capability* — here's how to do it — but capability was
never the scarce thing; deployment is. I watch companies buy the tool and never
operationalize it, and a self-serve course is that same failure one level up: you sell
someone the knowledge and they never wire it into how their business runs. So what the
packet's missing from where I sit is a baseline — nobody's measured whether people who
take a course like this actually *ship* anything. Until I see that, I can't tell you if
the course is a product or a very well-produced thing that changes nothing.

**The Margin Hawk (illustrative).**
The packet is missing the ceiling. One person billing hours is a flat rectangle, and
bespoke revenue resets to zero every month — you own nothing, you rent yourself out. What I
don't see anywhere is cost-of-production for the course against the revenue it throws off
after it's built once. That's the whole decision, and it's not on the table.

**The Craft Purist (illustrative).**
What I see first is the name on the door. Right now every outcome is controlled — in the
room, real data, capped promise. A self-serve course detaches the advice from the person
and ships it into a thousand rooms they'll never see, still on their name, and the packet
never says how you'd even *detect* a quality collapse at scale. You can't stand behind a
result you can't observe.

**The Distribution Maximalist (illustrative).**
The packet undersells timing. The audience is growing *now* and attention has a half-life.
A bespoke waitlist converts maybe one in fifty — the other forty-nine leave with nothing to
buy and learn to look elsewhere. Nothing here accounts for the funnel you burn every month
you have no product for the people who can't afford bespoke.

## Phase 3 — Proposals (one move each, with tradeoff and failure mode)

**Sawyer → Staged teaching probe.** A cheap paid cohort/workshop teaching what the practice
already does, near-zero new asset. *Tradeoff:* delays the scalable asset a quarter, won't
clear real revenue. *Failure mode:* proves people *pay to learn* but never checks whether
they *deploy* — you'd build on demand you measured and outcomes you didn't.

**Margin Hawk → Full self-serve build now.** Course as primary product this quarter, accept
a revenue dip. *Tradeoff:* gives up bespoke income and founder hours. *Failure mode:* a
quarter producing into a market where 95% die, no proof anyone wanted this one.

**Craft Purist → Pure bespoke, priced up.** No course; raise rates, cap the book, waitlist.
*Tradeoff:* non-bespoke audience unmonetized, ceiling nailed down. *Failure mode:* founder
stays the single point of failure; one bad capacity quarter and income drops.

**Distribution Maximalist → Free/cheap lead product now.** Ship free to capture the growing
audience, monetize later. *Tradeoff:* trains the audience to expect free, dilutes the
premium brand. *Failure mode:* you capture emails and nothing else — reach, no outcome.

## Phase 4 — Cross-Examination (seat versus seat, in voice)

**Margin Hawk vs. Craft Purist — scale against quality.**
> **Margin Hawk:** Your waitlist is a rectangle with a ceiling. You're defending a
> business model that can't grow past one person's calendar and calling it standards.
> **Craft Purist:** And you're proposing to put their name on a result you can't see
> and can't guarantee, to escape a ceiling. A course that ships bad outcomes at scale
> doesn't lift the ceiling — it takes the roof off the whole reputation. Scale a broken
> outcome and you've scaled the damage.

**Sawyer vs. Margin Hawk — cheap probe against the heavy build.**
> **Margin Hawk:** Your probe doesn't clear real revenue. A quarter of delay for a
> rounding error.
> **Sawyer:** That's the point — I'd spend a rounding error to *not* spend a quarter
> building the wrong asset. I've fallen for building the elaborate thing because it's fun
> that way, and that's not solving the problem by itself. Cheap test first — only if it
> proves people learn *and* deploy do we build the heavy thing. State it as a conditional
> and you can't lose the quarter.

The collision doesn't resolve: two seats want to move now, one wants to not move at all,
Sawyer recategorizes into "prove deployment cheaply first." Three moves survive to motions.

## Phase 5 — Motions

### Motion A — Staged teaching probe before any build
- **Sponsor** — Sawyer
- **Description** — Run one cheap paid teaching probe (small live cohort or workshop) this
  quarter, near-zero new asset, instrumented to measure two things separately: did people
  *pay*, and did they *deploy* what they learned.
- **Expected Benefit** — A real baseline on demand *and* outcomes before committing build
  hours — serves the Success Definition's quality-and-hours test.
- **Explicit Tradeoffs** — Delays the scalable asset ~a quarter; the probe won't clear
  meaningful revenue.
- **Risks** — A live cohort still costs founder-hours; a good result could tempt an
  over-built course.
- **Required Proof** — A measured deployment rate from participants, not a satisfaction
  score or a sales number.
- **Stop Conditions** — Participants pay but demonstrably don't deploy; or the probe eats
  more delivery capacity than it returns in learning.

### Motion B — Full self-serve build now
*(Same 8-field schema as Motion A, in brief.)* **Sponsor:** Margin Hawk *(illustrative)*.
**Description:** commit this quarter to building the course as primary product, accepting
a bespoke-revenue dip. **Benefit:** breaks the solo-hours ceiling if it lands.
**Tradeoffs:** sacrifices a quarter of billable delivery and founder attention.
**Risks:** builds into a crowded market with no demand proof — "95% will die."
**Required Proof:** paid conversions above a threshold within a launch window.
**Stop Conditions:** pre-sales below threshold; production overruns the quarter.

### Motion C — Pure bespoke, priced up
**Sponsor:** Craft Purist *(illustrative)*. **Description:** no product — raise rates, cap
the book, run a waitlist, protect outcome quality. **Benefit:** higher blended rate,
controlled outcomes, protected reputation. **Tradeoffs:** leaves the non-bespoke audience
unmonetized; ceiling stays. **Risks:** founder stays the single point of failure; income
tracks capacity. **Required Proof:** waitlist forms at the higher rate with no quality
complaints. **Stop Conditions:** waitlist doesn't form at the new rate; a capacity shock
drops income with no buffer.

## Phase 6 — Votes

### On Motion A (staged probe)
- **Sawyer — Yes.** Cheap test before heavy build; the only motion that puts a baseline
  under the deployment question instead of assuming it. *Disconfirming:* if a low-effort
  probe genuinely can't be instrumented for deployment — if all I can measure is "did they
  like it" — it's theater and I'd drop it.
- **Margin Hawk — Yes.** Cheap, and it de-risks the build I want. *Disconfirming:* the
  probe's delay costing more in lost compounding than the build's risk.
- **Craft Purist — Yes.** A small controlled cohort I can see is how you protect the
  outcome while you test. *Disconfirming:* the probe can't hold the quality bar with real
  participants.
- **Distribution Maximalist — Abstain.** Too slow to capture the funnel, but it doesn't
  burn the brand, so I won't block it. *Disconfirming:* proof the audience window is
  closing faster than a quarter.

### On Motion B (full build now)
- **Sawyer — No.** Heavy asset before the cheap test is the tool-first, build-for-fun
  mistake. *Disconfirming:* hard evidence of paid demand *and* deployment already in hand.
- **Margin Hawk — Yes.** The only motion that breaks the ceiling. *Disconfirming:* probe
  data showing people won't pay.
- **Craft Purist — No.** Ships my name into rooms I can't see with no proof of outcome.
  *Disconfirming:* a scalable way to observe and guarantee outcomes at a distance.
- **Distribution Maximalist — Yes.** A product beats no product. *Disconfirming:* evidence
  the audience won't convert to a paid course at all.

### On Motion C (pure bespoke)
- **Sawyer — No.** Protects the outcome, but nails the ceiling down forever and answers
  none of the question the packet asked. *Disconfirming:* the probe proving teaching
  *doesn't* produce deployment.
- **Margin Hawk — No.** It's the rectangle. *Disconfirming:* proof the course market can't
  be won at all.
- **Craft Purist — Yes.** The only motion that never risks the name. *Disconfirming:* a
  demonstrated way to scale without quality loss.
- **Distribution Maximalist — No.** It burns the funnel. *Disconfirming:* proof the
  audience won't pay for anything but bespoke.

**Tally.** A: 3 Yes, 1 Abstain — **adopted.** B: 2 Yes, 2 No — fails. C: 1 Yes, 3 No — fails.

## Phase 7 — Decision Record

- **decision** — Run a cheap paid teaching probe this quarter (Motion A) before building any
  self-serve asset, instrumented to measure demand and deployment separately. Treat the
  build-versus-bespoke question as *deferred and conditional* on the probe's deployment
  result, not settled.
- **rationale** — The real question underneath "course or bespoke" was never packaging; it
  was whether teaching produces deployed outcomes or only capability, and no seat could
  answer that from the packet because nobody had a baseline. Motion A is the only move that
  spends a rounding error to get one and stages every heavier commitment behind it.
- **refused** —
  - *Full self-serve build now (B)* — commits a quarter of build hours into a crowded market
    on unproven demand and unmeasured outcomes; buys the asset before knowing the outcome
    it's for.
  - *Pure bespoke forever (C)* — protects the outcome by permanently nailing the revenue
    ceiling and declining to answer the question the practice faces; a floor, not a decision.
- **dissent[]** —
  - **The Craft Purist (illustrative), on the deferral toward productization:** *"I voted
    for the probe, but on the record: a small controlled cohort is not a self-serve course.
    A satisfied cohort is not proof the unsupervised version works. If the probe 'succeeds'
    and we read that as a green light to detach the advice from the person, we measured the
    wrong thing and scaled the damage. Keep the name off anything I can't observe."*
- **proof** — **30-day proof-of-life metric:** the measured *deployment rate* among probe
  participants — the share who, within 30 days, wired what they learned into how their
  business runs (one shipped, operational change), not the share who paid or were satisfied.
  Above threshold, reopen toward "build"; at or below, toward "bespoke — teaching doesn't
  scale the outcome."
- **motions[]** — A (staged probe, adopted), B (full build now, failed), C (pure bespoke,
  failed).
- **votes{}** — seats ordered Sawyer · Margin Hawk · Craft Purist · Distribution Maximalist.
  - A → Yes · Yes · Yes · Abstain
  - B → No · Yes · No · Yes
  - C → No · No · Yes · No

## Doctrine Library — appended on close

**Principle adopted:** *Prove the outcome cheaply before you build the asset that scales
it.* When a decision is really "does this land?" wearing "how should we package it?", the
board funds the smallest probe that produces a baseline and stages every heavier commitment
behind that number — a productized version of expertise isn't adopted until there's measured
evidence the buyer *deploys*, not merely *consumes*, what it teaches. **Precedent for future
convenings:** re-diagnose any "scale versus craft" decision with no demand-and-outcome
baseline as a measurement problem before voting on packaging.
