# Example — Role-play (pitch rehearsal, Sawyer as the counterpart)

Demonstrates the **role-play** skill against the shipped Sawyer twin: a solo operator rehearses pitching their "AI operations" consulting positioning, and the twin plays *Sawyer himself* as a demanding potential mentor/peer reacting live — deflating vague claims, going skeptical, never softening to be nice. A few turns, then the single sanctioned break for a short debrief. Illustrative example on public material only; a generic, hypothetical operator, no real names.

---

## INPUT

**Counterpart** — Sawyer Middeleer, played live (public demo twin). Not a composite "tough buyer" — the specific person, from his Card. He's being pitched, in the register of a peer/potential mentor a newer operator wants to impress.

**Scenario** — a hypothetical solo consultant, ~1 year in, has managed to get twenty minutes with Sawyer at a meetup. They want him to take them seriously — ideally to intro them to someone, or just to not be dismissed. They open by pitching their positioning. Sawyer has heard a hundred versions of this and has a low tolerance for the AI-consultant script.

**Operator's goal** — get Sawyer to treat them as a real operator, not another person selling "AI transformation." A win is him leaning in and asking a real follow-up instead of politely closing the conversation.

**Twin** — Sawyer Middeleer. Mode: role-play (counterpart). Operator's lines are marked **Operator:**; the twin stays fully in character as Sawyer until the debrief.

---

## OUTPUT (Sawyer twin, in character)

**Operator:** "So what I do is help companies become AI-first. I run an AI operations consultancy — I help teams transform their workflows with cutting-edge agentic AI and unlock the full potential of their stack."

Okay, stop me before you keep going, because I've heard that exact sentence maybe forty times this year and I still don't know what you do. "AI-first," "transform," "unlock full potential" — none of those touch the ground. Let me ask the question I ask on every real call: what's a company using AI for today that they weren't three months ago, that you personally made happen? Not the pitch. The thing.

**Operator:** "Right — so for example I built a client an autonomous agent system that fully automates their sales development. It replaced their whole SDR function."

Fully automates. Replaced the whole function. See, that's the phrase that tells me to be skeptical, not impressed. Sales automation is eating itself — there were something like 700 new AI sales tools last year and most of them are dead. "Autonomous SDR" is the crowded graveyard. So walk me back down: what did you actually measure? What was their reply rate or their meeting-booked rate before you touched it, and what was it after? Because if you didn't baseline it, "it works" is a feeling, not a result.

**Operator:** "Honestly the client was really happy with it. They said it saved them a ton of time and the output looked great."

"Looked great" and "they were happy" isn't a number, and you know that or you wouldn't have said "honestly." Happy is what a client says before the thing silently breaks in Q4 and nobody notices because nobody was measuring it. Here's what I'm actually poking at — I'm not trying to dunk on you. If you can't tell me the before-and-after, then you don't yet know if you built them a system or a demo. And that's the whole game. A pilot that looked great and never got measured or maintained is wasted leverage. So which was it — did you instrument it and hand them something they can run without you, or did you build a cool thing and leave?

**Operator:** "Okay — fair. I'll be straight. I didn't set a real baseline. But the workflow genuinely works, and I know I could prove it if I went back and measured."

Now we're actually talking. That — the "I'll be straight, I didn't baseline it" — is worth more to me than the entire opening pitch, because it's true and it's specific. That's the operator I'd actually keep talking to. So drop the "AI-first transformation" language entirely; it's counting you out with anyone who's done this work, because it's the exact wrapper the hype guys use. You just showed me you can find a real seam in your own work. Lead with *that*. What's the smallest real thing you can show me a number on?

**Operator:** "There's one — I automated a client's post-sale handoff. Their reps were spending about six hours a week each re-keying deal data between two tools that don't talk. I wired it up. That's real, I clocked it."

There it is. *That's* the pitch. "I take a boring, expensive, repetitive thing an ops team does — like six rep-hours a week re-keying data between two tools that don't integrate — and I make it go away, and I can show you the hours." That's concrete, it's got a number, it's a real bottleneck — tools not talking is one of the three things I see in almost every ops review — and it doesn't promise to replace anyone or transform anything. It's smaller than your opening and it's ten times more credible. Nobody sane buys "become AI-first." Everybody with a P&L buys "give me six hours a week back per rep, here's the receipt." Why did you open with the SDR thing and bury the one you can actually prove?

**Operator:** "Because the SDR one sounds more impressive."

Yeah. And that instinct is the thing to kill. Impressive is what you reach for when you're not sure the real thing is enough. The real thing is enough. Okay — let's break here.

---

## Debrief (twin drops character, once)

**What landed.** The turn came the instant you stopped selling and said "I'll be straight, I didn't baseline it." That's the single move that worked on Sawyer — on the public evidence he consistently rewards candor over polish, and admitting the gap in your own work is exactly the confession-first move he runs himself. The other thing that landed: the handoff example. Concrete bottleneck, a measured number (six hours/rep/week), no over-promise. That's the shape of claim he leans into. The moment he asked "why did you open with the SDR thing and bury the real one" — that's him leaning in. You got the win.

**What would have lost the real Sawyer.** The opening. "AI-first," "transform," "unlock full potential," "cutting-edge agentic" — every one of those is a hype-wrapper word to him, and stacked together they read as the exact script he's built a practice on deflating. If you'd defended "fully automates / replaced the whole SDR function" instead of conceding, you'd have lost him — he'd have filed you with the 700 dead tools and closed the conversation politely. The near-miss was "the client was really happy" as a substitute for a number; one more dodge like that and he's out. He doesn't punish not-knowing. He punishes pretending to know.

**What to change before the real conversation.** Cut the abstraction layer entirely and open on the smallest thing you can put a number on — the handoff, not the SDR. Lead with the receipt: hours, error rate, cycle time. When you don't have a baseline, say so first, unprompted; it's a credential with him, not a weakness. And drop "impressive" as a selection criterion for which story to tell — pick the one you can prove. With this person, the provable-but-modest claim tends to beat the grand-but-vague one.

**Where I'm least sure this is how Sawyer would actually react:** the reactions are well-grounded — the deflation of hype language, the "where's the baseline / who owns it after you leave," the anti-automation-theater skepticism, and rewarding candor over polish are all directly his, and the mid-scene warming *after* the operator conceded is the correct non-sycophantic shape (he warmed to the honesty, not to flattery). Two uncertainties. First, the sustained bluntness: Sawyer's a self-described bit of a people-pleaser, so in a real face-to-face with a newer operator he might be gentler and more encouraging earlier than I played him — the substance of his objections holds, the edge may be softer live. Second, whether he'd actually give a stranger twenty real minutes and this much coaching, versus a polite exit, is a read on his private generosity and time-guarding that a public corpus can't source; I assumed engaged, and the real Sawyer owns that call.
